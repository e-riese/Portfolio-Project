  1. Introduction

This analysis has the intention of verifying the research conducted in Acemoglu, et al. (2008) which investigates the relationship between income on democracy. Income levels for decades have been championed by the modernization hypothesis as a key causal effect into creating democratic societies. Acemoglu, et al. (2008) found that there is little evidence to support this. A positive correlation between the two was acknowledged, but it was also speculated that causational variables for democracy are most likely explained by other factors. In their research they also briefly mention that they conducted regressions with non-linear interactions between income and other variables, but did not report the results. They found that there was no evidence for any relationships. As an extension of their research, this paper will analyze if there is non-linear relationship between income and democracy.

  1. The Context and Data[1]

The measure of democracy used in this report is the Augmented Freedom House Political Rights Index developed by Acemoglu, et al (2008), and is the response variable for this research. It is the Freedom House Political Rights Index from the Freedom House Organization. It is supplemented with related data from Kenneth A. Bollen (1990, 2001) for certain years. This index is also supplemented with data from the Polity IV dataset and the Polity’s Democracy and Autocracy indices (see Monty G. Marshall and Keith Jaggers 2004). The indices are transformed to be between 0 and 1 with one being the most democratic. The main explanatory variable used is the log real GDP per capita lagged to fit the time frames of the panel data appropriately. The real GDP is in parity purchasing power and is taken from the Penn World database. Other supplementary explanatory variables include average years of schooling, percentage of the population in certain age groups (the groups are specified in Table 1) and   the log of total population. All these variables had to be lag adjusted to accurate demonstrate the timing of the effects of variables. The type of data used is panel data where the significance of year-fixed and country-fixed effects are taken into account, in addition to robust standard error clustering. The sample period is from 1960 to 2000 and each observation corresponds to 5-year intervals. 147 countries are used in the subsample for this paper.

  1. I - Base Specification Regression Analysis Results[2] 

Regression 1 is the econometric model for the Augmented Freedom House Political Rights Index regressed on the five-year lagged log of Real GDP per Capita. This model predicts that for every 1% increase in the GDP per capita, there should be an increase in the Augmented Freedom House Political Rights Index by 0.00225 points[3] holding all other independent variables constant. This is quite a small change, especially when considering that this value represents only a 0.2% change in the democracy index. The p-value for this estimation suggests that the estimate is statistically significant at the 1% level. The confidence interval suggests that 95% of the time, for a 1% increase in the GDP per capita, we would expect the average increase in the index to be between 0.00201 and 0.00250. This narrow interval suggests a high degree of certainty in this estimate.

It appears that when solely looking at the effect of income on democracy, the regression in itself suggest that there is a statistically significant relationship between the two variables. This however is not a fully accurate representation of the situation. To fairly account for the sampling methodology in the model, it is necessary to take into account certain controls. Regressions 2 through 6 gradually introduce new controls which may also affect the relationship between income and democracy.

Only 147 countries have been sampled in this regression, but it is intended to be able to draw conclusions about the greater population, which in this context is every single country. To account for this, key controls include clustering the standard errors at the country level. These were included in Regression 2 and onwards. The estimates do not change from Regression 1 after introducing standard errors clustered at country levels, and the estimate remains statistically significant at the 1% level. The confidence interval however does widen slightly, and the standard error increases, suggesting slightly less certainly in the results. The results do not need to change drastically for this step to be necessary for fairer results. It seems quite likely that this model suffers from omitted variable bias, since democracy is such a complex variable with many underlying variables which may affect it. A time invariant factor that varies with democracy and income systematically may be certain country-specific characteristics (e.g. climate, geography, culture etc.) which would significantly bias results.

Regression 3 includes fixed effects for countries in the sample. It predicts that for every 1% increase in the GDP per capita, there should be an increase in the Augmented Freedom House Political Rights Index by 0.00055 points holding all other independent variables constant. This is even smaller (less than half) of what was predicted in the previous two regressions, and it is only statistically significant at the 10% level. The standard error has also increased by more than half the previous regression. The confidence interval suggests that 95% of the time, for a 1% increase in the GDP per capita, we would expect the average increase in the index to be between -0.00005 and 0.00120. This is a questionable result since it suggests the average democracy levels may increase or decrease 95% of the time, and the interval is much larger suggesting less certainty. Fixed country specific factors have been controlled for in this regression, yet controls for time varying confounders has not. Without this it is likely that there is omitted variable bias occurring in the regression since panel data is being used. There may be factors which do not change over time, but still affect democracy and income (e.g. certain economic or diplomatic agreements that multiple countries have agreed to over decades).

Regression 4 includes year fixed effects. It predicts that for every 1% increase in the GDP per capita, there should be an increase in the Augmented Freedom House Political Rights Index by 0.00059 points holding all other independent variables constant. This is marginally larger than the estimate from Regression 3, but still quite a small change in the overall index. The standard error has once again increased, and the coefficient estimate does not appear to be statistically significant even at the 10% level. The confidence interval suggests that 95% of the time, for a 1% increase in the GDP per capita, we would expect the average increase in the index to be between -0.0024 and 0.0142. So once again there is less certainty if the change in the index will go up or down. The exclusion of the year fixed effects was tested with an F-test. The F-test result was 6.76 with a statistically significant p-value at the 1% level. This indicates that the hypothesis that the coefficients for all years are jointly insignificant should be rejected. Therefore, there is statistical evidence that year fixed effects are required in this model to get valid results.

        Regression 5 looks at the effect of certain demographic controls, specifically age groups, education and log population. Age was regressed as a dummy variable with the base case being the middle age group consisting of people aged 30-45. A subsample of the data was used for this regression since missing data was omitted, and the same specifications as Regression 4 were conducted on the subsample. This model predicted that for every 1% increase in the GDP per capita, there should be a decrease in the Augmented Freedom House Political Rights Index by 0.00023 points holding all other independent variables constant. Income is now predicted to cause a decrease in the democracy which is conflicts with the modernization hypothesis that suggests the opposite. The magnitude of the coefficient is much smaller once again, suggesting there is less change in the democracy index. The p-value is also not statistically significant even at the 10% level. The regression also predicts that 95% of the time, for a 1% increase in the GDP per capita, it is expected that the average increase in the democracy index should be between -0.00120 and 0.00076. Again, this is a questionable result since it suggests the democracy levels may go up or down given income and the interval has widened. Testing the exclusion of the year-fixed effects, once again the p-value is significant at the 1% level. There is thus statistical evidence that year fixed effects are required in this model to get valid results. It appears so far, as we begin to add more controls and more independent variables proposed by the modernization hypothesis, the less statistically significant and less coherent the results from the regression become.

         The final regression does not use the subsample to run the regression, but automatically omits missing data and obtains slightly different results. This model predicted that for every 1% increase in the GDP per capita, there should be an increase in the Augmented Freedom House Political Rights Index by 0.00016 points holding all other independent variables constant. This is the smallest predicted coefficient and has significantly decreased from Regression 5. It now implies that income should increase democracy rather than the reverse found in Regression 5, and, this estimate is still not statistically significant at the 10% level. This all suggests a lack of robustness for income as a predictor of democracy.

The exclusion of the year fixed effects, age controls, and demographic controls (education and log population), were each tested respectively. Year fixed effects were found to be statistically significant at the 1% level and age controls were found to be statistically significant at the 10% level. The demographic controls which included education and population however were found to not be significant even at the 10% level. This suggests that education and population size do not affect the democracy index in a statistically significant way and should be excluded from the model. Conversely, this suggests that year fixed effects and age controls are required for this model to get valid results.

This model predicts that for every average schooling year increase, there will be a decrease in the Augmented Freedom House Political Rights Index by 0.004 points holding all other independent variables constant. For the log population value, it suggests that a 1% increase in the log population would imply a decrease in the index by -0.00104 points, when compared to a 1% increase in the middle age population. Neither estimate was statistically significant, and both very small estimates given the scale of the data. This further suggests that education and population are not relevant to explaining the democracy index or that there may be some multicollinearity error in these variables.

All the age dummy variables appeared to be statistically significant at the 10% level or lower when compared to the base case of a middle-aged population. It suggests that a 1% increase in population of very young people would imply an increase in the index by 2.618 more, then when compared to a 1% increase in the middle age population. For young, old, and very old populations it suggests similarly a 1% increase in each of these populations holding all other independent variables constant would lead to a 2.109, 3.070 and 3.466 greater increase in the index respectively, when compared to a 1% increase in the middle age population.

        It appears that there is an overarching pattern; as a more accurate regression model is constructed for income and democracy, the results gradually become more statistically insignificant. This agrees with the results found in Acemoglu, et al (2008). They found that there is not much statistical evidence to support a causal relationship between income and democracy, in addition to other variables related to the tenets of the modernization hypothesis.

II - Base Specification Regression Analysis MLR Assumptions

We have assumed that the true population model is linear in parameters. Given the nature of the panel series data it is difficult to check with a scatter plot if there is a linear relationship due to clusters of data corresponding to year and countries. The previous research from Acemoglu, et al (2008) assumes the data to be linear in parameters, but does so in order to build on past research to verify the model’s appropriateness in other aspects. The same is done in this report, but the extension will examine other possible relationships. It is one of the weaker assumptions so it would not heavily skew the results to assume this.

        The sampling was not done completely randomly. The same number of observations was gathered for each country and time frame such that a fair weighted representation of these variables was chosen. Having more or less data from one country or one specific year would skew the data. The random sampling assumption does not hold completely, but it was necessary to choose the data as such to prevent bias. The specific time frames and countries observations have necessary random components such as the averaged values for demographics variables. Random sampling is also a weak assumption. Given this, the assumption not holding fully will not severely affect the results.

        By quickly opening the data set it is evident that there are no constant values for any of the independent variables. There also does not appear to be any exact linear relationship between the independent variables since no variable is directly comprised of one another. Although individual F-tests were conducted, a table of correlations between all the independent variables was also constructed (See Table 4). It appears that all, apart from the young, age group variables and the education variable are highly correlated above 70% with other independent variables, but none are perfectly correlated.[4] It is likely that the no perfect collinearity assumption holds.

        The zero conditional mean is a strong assumption that is impossible to ever conclusively assume. However, by trying multiple regressions with different independent variables, it is possible to get close to a true answer by examining the effects and effectiveness of different independent variable combinations to determine omitted variable bias. Based on the nature of the panel data used in this analysis, the year-fixed effects and the country-fixed effects needed to be included to account for clustering and allow a fair representation across different countries and time. When these variables are included in the model real GDP per capita became statistical insignificant.[5] Yet the F-test suggests that the year-fixed effects are necessary for modelling correctly. This suggests that income does not affect democracy in the way suggested by the modernization hypothesis. Or, if so, there may be other key variables that are missing which work in conjunction with income to do so. These are most likely neither total population nor education level since their F-tests also were statistically insignificant. It is thus assumed these two variables should be removed from the model.

Overall, it seems appropriate to use a multiple linear regression model given that attention is paid to the statistical significance of certain explanatory variables added.

  1. Extension Regression Analysis Results[6] 

To further the research of Acemoglu, et al. (2008), the square of income was included in the regression. The was first with demographic controls, and then without when their F-test deemed them still statistically insignificant similar to the base specification they were removed. Regression 7 predicted that for every 1% increase in the GDP per capita, there should be a decrease in the Augmented Freedom House Political Rights Index by 0.00160 points holding all other independent variables constant. It also predicted that for every 1% increase in the GDP per capita, there should be an increase in the Augmented Freedom House Political Rights Index by 0.00002 points holding all other independent variables constant. These results were both not statistically significant even at the 10% level, and took into account all the same controls as regression 6. These changes overall are also very small, particularly the squared income variable. The confidence interval for the squared income implies that 95% of the time, for a 1% increase in the GDP per capita, we would expect the average increase in the index to be between -0.00049 and 0.00053. This interval is narrow given that the standard error is 0.00026, which suggests a high degree of certainty in this estimate. That being said the fact that 95% of the time the average change in the index could go up or down doesn’t seem like a very conclusive result. This evidence further demonstrates that there is no relationship between income and democracy beyond just a linear relationship.

        Essentially the exact same pattern was found in regression 8, but the lack of demographic controls changed the coefficient estimates slightly. Removing the demographic controls also lowered the standard errors significantly. Regression 8 predicted that for every 1% increase in the GDP per capita, there should be a decrease in the Augmented Freedom House Political Rights Index by 0.00146 points holding all other independent variables constant. It also predicted that for every 1% increase in the GDP per capita, there should be an increase in the Augmented Freedom House Political Rights Index by 0.00013 points holding all other independent variables constant. These results were both not statistically significant even at the 10% level, and took into account all the same controls as regression 6. These changes overall are also very small, particularly the squared income variable. Its confidence interval implies that 95% of the time, for a 1% increase in the GDP per capita, we would expect the average increase in the index to be between -0.0021 and 0.0048. This interval is narrow given that the standard error is 0.00018, which suggests a high degree of certainty in this estimate. This confidence interval also implies that the average change in the index could go up or down, which doesn’t seem like a very conclusive result.

        These results adhere to the brief mention of a similar process in Acemoglu, et al. (2008). There still does not appear to be any relationship between income and democracy even with a non-linear model.

  1. Limitations of Results

The main findings of this research show that there is no strong evidence of a relationship between income and democracy, linear or quadratic. It is still possible that another type of non-linear relationship may fit the data better, however Acemoglu, et al. (2008) also briefly mentions trying nonmonotonic effects which also yielded no statistically significant relationship.

In one journal article by Bartolucci and Moral-Benito (2012) they perform the same analysis on income and income squared using Acemoglu, et al. (2008) as a starting point as was done here. They found similar results where income squared had no effect on democracy. They did however, find that when they made the conditions more stringent, by comparing low-income countries to high-income countries, that a pattern would emerge. Low-income countries experienced a significant non-linear effect of income on democracy even after controlling for country specific effects. After a certain threshold of income was attain this effect would vanish. This is significant evidence that there may be an underlying relationship between income and democracy but in very specific situations which indicates that the zero mean condition is most likely not held in the base specifications and extended research.

  1. Conclusion

The underlying mechanisms for the development of democracy are multifaceted and deeply complex. Using regression as a tool to identify causal relationships is often tricky to navigate when it can only conclusively draw correlative ones. With the base specification regressions it seems that there isn’t much to suggest that income causes democracy, just as was concluded in Acemoglu, et al. (2008). Their relationship is most appropriately deemed correlational, and it seems some other explanation must be out there for what creates a democracy. It appears that a quadratic relationship also does not yield statistically significant results between income and democracy. However, there is additional research out there, such as Bartolucci and Moral-Benito (2012), which suggests in certain cases there may be a significant link between the two. Income may have a non-linear effect for low-income countries, then when a certain threshold for a country’s income is reached the effect disappears. With that in mind, it seems that it may be possible that there are special cases of causal income effects on democracy which have yet to be touched upon by Acemoglu, et al. (2008), and these effects appear to be much more subtle and may only exhibit in more clandestine circumstances.
