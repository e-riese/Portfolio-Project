Introduction 

COVID-19 has left a monumental mark on individuals, societies, and economies worldwide. Vaccine hesitancy has steadily increased due to the spread of misinformation, concerns of accelerated vaccine development, and possible adverse side effects (Adhikari and Cheah 2021). To keep Canadians safe from the disease, health officials should try to identify which population groups are experiencing vaccine hesitancy to spread proper awareness.

The purpose of this research paper is to determine whether education, gender and age are significant in predicting vaccine acceptance for COVID-19 in Canada by applying machine learning methods. It is hypothesized that the demographic factors of education, gender and age will be significant in predicting vaccine acceptance in Canada, and that a decision tree can accurately classify an individual's traits to their vaccine acceptance preference.

The majority of current research utilizes open-ended surveys or demographic population proportions to analyze vaccine hesitancy (Public Health Agency of Canada 2021). However, there is one notable survey that drew conclusions using logarithmic regression models and quantitative population percentage methods. It was conducted by the Strategic Advisory Group of Experts on Immunization, a research group formed by the WHO (Lazarus et al. 2020) in 2020. Our paper intends to contribute to current research by applying a decision tree model to the survey data in order to generate predictive models to understand vaccine hesitancy.

Data & Methodology 

The data was sourced from the 2021 WHO research paper “A Global Survey of Potential Acceptance of a COVID-19 Vaccine” (Lazarus et al., 2020). It was collected from June 16thto June 20th 2020 from an online panel of 13,426 respondents. The survey respondents represent a

random sample of the top 19 countries affected most by COVID-19, including Canada. Participants were recruited through varying online panel providers to avoid coverage bias and verified using mobile numbers or IP addresses to confirm real and unique responses.

The raw data was cleaned by removing non-Canadian entries and unrelated predictors such as race, language, COVID-19 cases, and COVID-19 deaths. Although “Income” was an initial predictor used in the analysis, it was removed due to translation issues and conversion rates. Responses labeled “Prefer Not To Answer”, “NA” or empty cells were grouped as “0” to maintain other useful data points. Next, variable names were changed for simplicity and columns were converted to numeric values where applicable. Predictor variables included “education,” “gender” and “age” with “willingness to get vaccinated” as the response variable. The variables were converted into categorical values from the survey, with the response following the five point Likert scale. Participants were asked to respond with the following: ‘completely agree’, ‘ somewhat agree’, ‘no opinion’, ‘somewhat disagree’ and ‘completely disagree’. The ‘completely agree’ and ‘somewhat agree’ responses were grouped and encoded as “would get vaccinated”, ‘completely disagree’ and ‘somewhat disagree’ grouped as “would not get vaccinated”, and neutral as a singular category. Age groups were categorized as 0:‘Prefer Not To Say’, 1:‘18-24’, 2:‘25-54’, 3:‘55-64’, 4:‘65 years or older’. Education was categorized as, 0:‘Prefer Not To Say’, 1:‘Less Than High School’, 2:‘High School/Some College’, 3:‘Bachelor’s Degree’ and 4:‘Postgraduate Degree’. Gender was encoded as 0:‘Prefer Not to Say’, 1:‘Male’, 2:‘Female’, 3:‘Transgender’ and 4:’Other’. Table 1 and Table 2 summarize significant descriptive variable statistics with the bar charts showing the frequencies of variables (refer to Appendix A). The bubble scatterplots also aid in visualizing correlations between the categorical predictor variables against the categorical response variable (refer to Appendix B).

A correlation matrix was plotted to interpret the correlation coefficient between the variables. The highest coefficient of 0.36 was found between education level and vaccine acceptance with the lowest coefficient of 0.02 between gender and vaccine acceptance. The data was then split into a training and testing set, with respective ratios of 70% to 30%. Next, “Logistic Regression” “Decision Tree Classification”, “K-nn Classifier” and “Naive Bayes” were used to model the training data and given the test set to evaluate accuracy. The “Decision Tree Classification” outputted the highest training and testing accuracy rates and was subsequently used to classify our model (Refer to Appendix C).

Since the variables are all categorical data, the data types needed to be converted to numeric values accordingly. “One-Hot Encoding” was performed to transform the integer values to dummy variables using the “get_dummies” function in pandas. The data was then split into train/test sets and fit to the classification tree and a preliminary plot was visualized. The preliminary tree was evidently overfitting the training set, containing an overwhelming number of branches. The confusion matrix for the preliminary tree was also plotted with an accuracy rate of 65% and an error rate of 35%. 

Decision trees are notorious for overfitting so we will tune the parameters by finding the optimal pruning parameter alpha to improve predictive accuracy, known as pruning. Cross-validation was used to find optimal alpha, beginning with the standard value of “CV = 10”. The range of optimal alpha was found by plotting the accuracy of the tree as a function of different alpha values, for both training and testing sets. The various alpha values were then stored in a list, with the maximum alpha omitted since it is just the root, and visualized in a plot. The optimal value in the graph is approximately 0.003 (refer to Appendix D) but was verified by finding the highest accuracy rate in a range of (0.002, 0.005). The final optimal value for alpha

The pruned decision tree confusion matrix indicates the tuned parameter performed better at classifying since the accuracy rate was higher (See Appendix D). It shifted from 65% to 68% accuracy.

Results 

The bubble scatter plots indicate those with “Less Than High School” tend to disagree with receiving a vaccine, with higher education attainment correlating to higher vaccine willingness indicated by the dark green circles. There is also an association between younger age groups of ‘18-24’ and ‘25-54’, with higher vaccine willingness. It is hypothesized that individuals who are more educated have a thorough understanding of vaccination technology and are more knowledgeable of the benefits. Similarly, younger age groups may be exposed to this information from the influx of social media, new sources, and interconnection through peers. There are no significant correlations between gender and vaccination acceptance based on the scatterplots. Correlations are further supported by the correlation matrix with a coefficient of 0.36 between education and vaccination hesitancy.

By implementing methods of Logistics Regression, Decision Trees, KNN, Naive Bayes, our accuracy rates for our training and testing sets range from 56% - 65% which are quite significant. Ultimately, the accuracy rates from the decision tree were highest (refer to Appendix C). After tuning our parameters of the decision tree, the confusion matrix produced an accuracy rate of 68% with an error rate of 32% which is remarkably better than the unpruned model (refer to Figure D3 and D4). In summary, our pruned decision tree is able to model demographics of education, age, and gender and classify an individual to their respective vaccination preference.

Conclusion 

From our analysis, education and age play a significant role with gender being insignificant in predicting vaccination preferences. Although willingness to receive a vaccine is not exactly the same as going out to get vaccinated, similar to the Lazarus et al. 2020 paper it is assumed to be a relatively good metric of such. It seems that a decision tree can reasonably predict who is likely to be vaccine hesitant given certain demographic metrics.

In every study there are various limitations of the analysis. Since the original dataset was surveyed on a global scale, information on Canadians may have been undersampled to the true population. A few observations included "Prefer Not To Answer" or empty data which may misrepresent a target demographic. Additionally, this data was extracted June 2020 where many individuals may have been skeptical of vaccination technology or unaware of the severe impact. Furthermore, human error may have played a part in misinterpreting the questions asked, collecting the data, or inputting it into a database.

The Canadian government may address the elderly adult population and those who possess education levels below high school, target groups who experience vaccine hesitancy. Ultimately, applying statistical methods and inferencing trends to demographic groups is beneficial in protecting our global health.
