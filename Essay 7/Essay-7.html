The Subtle Influence of Adolphe Quetelet’s “l’homme moyen (The Average Man)”
The normal distribution began its development most notably with Carl Gauss’ "Theoria
combinationis observationum erroribus minimis obnoxiae" (Theory of the combination of
observations least subject to error) in 1823. Its applications at that point, as described by Gauss,
were limited to measurement error calculations for astronomical observations. It was then Adolphe
Quetelet who pioneered the application of the normal distribution in other fields of study outside
of astronomy as it is more commonly used today (Jahoda 2015). Throughout his career he
advocated for its application in social sciences and worked towards developing statistical sciences
as an academic field. His 1835 work; “Sur l'homme et le développement de ses facultés, ou Essai
de physique sociale” (On Man and the Development of his Faculties, or Essays on Social Physics),
was one of the first to apply the normal distribution to identify information about populations
(Jahoda 2015). The most influential concept from this book is that of “l’homme moyen” (The
Average Man). Quetelet describes “The Average Man” as an ideal fictitious concept of the
quintessential human being in all aspects i.e. intelligence, beauty, stature, morality etc. He used
the normal distribution to find mean estimates different aspects of the population to form
measurements of “The Average Man”. He believed that these average values must be naturally
inherent to human beings and were strictly true as scientific laws (Adolphe Quetelet [1842] 1968).
“The Average Man” in essence is a combination of objective statistical tools that calculate relevant
means, and philosophical concepts of human nature which give real world meanings of those
values. Its uses and influence have evolved over time due to the shifting paradigms of how we
understand human nature and because it is conceptually a blend of mathematical and philosophical
ideas. This essay aims to advocate for the uncelebrated historical importance of “The Average
Man” by analyzing its influence in the statistics field and deconstructing how and why its influence

Approximately one century earlier to Quetelet’s work, Newtonianism completely changed
peoples’ understanding of the universe. People began to understand the world as being dictated in
an innate and fundamentally consistent way. These deterministic beliefs were in direct opposition
of the views held by most Victorians at the time, for whom denial of freewill undermined basic
tenets of their Christian beliefs. This direct contrast between society’s cultural roots in faith and
the objectivity of Newtonianism is what gave rise to the key philosophical debate of freewill versus
determinism. One of the key influences of “The Average Man” was its use as empirical evidence
for scholars and academics in this debate (Jahoda 2015). Quetelet’s himself held the deterministic
belief that “The Average Man” must be as innate as Newtonian laws of motion, given that the
results were similarly based in mathematical rigor and as consistent. Quetelet’s work included a
section on criminology which demonstrated the consistency of crime frequency and type by certain
population groups (Adolphe Quetelet [1842] 1968). This is the main result which led religious
Victorian society at the time to think that Quetelet was questioning the existence of an individual’s
freewill (Jahoda 2015). Henry Thomas Buckle, one of the most significant scientific historians of
the 19
th
century was a key figure in the discussions on determinism versus freewill. He argued that
history is also subject to deterministic laws in a scientific manner as seen in examples of “The
Average Man” and so the subject should be treated as a physical science (Henry Thomas Buckle
[1857] 1908). Many other famous scholars then engaged in academic discussion on Buckle’s and
Quetelet’s deterministic arguments including Charles Darwin, John Stuart Mill and Clerk Maxwell
(Jahoda 2015). Although not in the direct spotlight of the debate now, “The Average Man” was at

Another scholar of the time most notably influenced by “The Average Man” was British
polymath Francis Galton. In his 1869 book entitled “Hereditary Genius” he discusses statistical
methods from “The Average Man” then appropriates them to his own research in eugenics. This
includes use of the normal distribution on populations, Quetelet’s research into crime statistics and
average measurements of the human body. Galton’s work in eugenics made attempts to measure
intelligence in order to rank the evolutionary superiority of races with grade scores and made links
between race and Quetelet’s criminology work (Tafreshi 2021). Galton was also the cousin of
famous scientist Charles Darwin and was significantly influenced by his cousin’s work in
evolution. Much of Galton’s work in eugenics tried to link the newfound hypothesis of evolution
to race (Fancher 2009). Our modern understanding of genetics proves that the segregation of
human beings into race is solely a societal construct, with no grounds in scientific or biological
reasoning (Duello et al. 2021). If we dissect the historical and political context of 1869 and
Galton’s background, one can see how Galton came to these bigoted conclusions. Slavery abolition
movements had slowly gained traction in Europe and the Americas, with the American Civil War
ending four years prior in 1865. However, colonialism in Africa was growing with essentially the
entire continent under European control by the turn of the century. Racist beliefs were
conventional and collectively justified by citing Europe’s global political supremacy. Emancipated
slaves still lacked basic rights, and the longstanding cultures of racial bigotry did not
instantaneously change with the laws of abolition and is still taking centuries to be undone (Niels
Eichhorn 2019). The 19

Victorian nurse and statistician Florence Nightingale, like the other scholars mentioned so
far, studied Quetelet’s work “The Average Man”. The wider societal implications drawn from
“The Average Man” inspired Nightingale’s use of statistical methods in the medical field (Jahoda
2015). She used statistical methods to track different causes of soldiers’ death during the Crimean
War. She discovered that most were dying from diseases spreading through the abhorrent living
conditions rather than the actual fighting. She pushed for sanitary reforms based on her data. These
reforms are thought to be one of the reasons behind the population boom in the later 19
th
century
into the 20
th
in England (Nightingale and Mcdonald 2001). Like Quetelet, she then became an
advocate for statistical sciences as a serious academic field and its applications in public health.
Nightingale had attended a Statistical Conference in 1860 in London where Quetelet was also in
attendance. They met at the conference and continued correspondence for many years. He was also
known to personally send her copies of his later statistical manuscripts as gifts. She described
Quetelet as the creator of statistics, for his foresight into its applications, and was very much
influenced by him especially in the later years of her life (Jahoda 2015).
Statistical science was still developing into a full-fledged academic field over the course
of the 19
th
century, with Quetelet being one of the first to suggest it as a new field in of itself. He
coined the term “social physics” to describe the use of the normal distribution on population groups
to study information about them. “The Average Man” was his most known work in “social
physics” and is an early example of a sociological statistics-based study. Many science historians
today cite his work in “social physics” as one of the origins of sociology as an academic field also
(Aubin 2014). The influence of “The Average Man” evidently permeated a variety of academic

Although today most people would not have heard of Adolphe Quetelet, they would have
heard of the body mass index (BMI) measurement. BMI is a board way to categorize if someone
is underweight, normal weight or overweight based on the ratio of their mass to their height
squared. The idea of measuring human size using mass and height squared was first developed
mathematically in “The Average Man” but popularized in the 1972 by psychologist Ancel Keys.
It was Keys and his research team that came up with the name “body mass index”, however the
measure was originally called “The Quetelet Index” after its creator Adolphe Quetelet. Before
Keys popularized the use of BMI, it was common practice to measure body fat with skin calipers
and body density with underwater weighing. Keys and his team felt that “The Quetelet Index” was
better than these older methods and advocated for its use in their research, all while renaming it
the “body mass index”. They commented that Quetelet never intended for his ratio index to be
used to measure body fat nor body size in order to justify using the exact same ratio as “The
Quetelet Index”. Despite discussing Quetelet’s work in their research, they failed to directly cite

On a much briefer note of the modern influence of “The Average Man”, the most timeless
concept of this work must be that of using averages to measure populations. It seems difficult today
to imagine the field of statistics without doing this. Yet, this fundamental concept of “The Average
Man” is really what most statistical population investigations today still strive for (Jahoda 2015).
The end goal of many statistical investigations is often a range of different measures, but the most
pertinent is really the average of a population. Other pertinent statistical measures are generally
further descriptors of the mean, such as variance (i.e., deviation from the mean) and confidence
interval (i.e., how frequently the calculated mean would appear after many trials) (A Colin
Cameron and Trivedi 2005). For every population measured by statistics, the origin of these
methods is fundamentally and often unbeknownst grounded in “The Average Man”.
When one reflects upon historical figures in statistics, Quetelet’s name may not be
considered the most famous. Yet, when one thoroughly examines the influence of “The Average
Man” over time from its publication to the modern day, it appears that it has a more predominant
historical influence than one may have originally thought. Much of his work was quoted, inspired
or appropriated by more well scholars and academics with little credit due to Quetelet. “The
Average Man” began by inspiring pioneering the application of statistical methods on populations,
sparking philosophical debates and inspiring fellow statisticians of the time to pioneer the field.
As time went on the last direct remnants of his work are found in the BMI measurement, and the
persistence of measuring and defining populations with averages. Science is sometimes feigned as
the few miraculous discoveries of great people, but in actuality it is a slow and meticulous process.
It is the accumulation and collaboration of many peoples’ works across space and time and the
