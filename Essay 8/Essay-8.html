Deconstructing Eurocentric Historical Mathematical Perspectives: The Role of the Islamic
World in Transmission of Hindu-Arabic Numerals to Western Civilization
1
The classic Eurocentric view of mathematics would argue that the introduction of the
Hindu-Arabic system to Western Civilization should be most significantly attributed to
Leonardo Fibonacci through his 1202 work Liber Abaci (in English, The Book of
Calculation). It suggests that Fibonacci was the first to realise the greatness behind the Hindu-
Arabic system, and that the story of how this system arrived in Europe would start and end
with Fibonacci.
1
In actuality this is an oversimplification of historic events. Although his work
certainly was imperative to the popularization of the numeracy system, it lacks the full picture
of how the system truly got to Europe; in addition to how it developed over time and the
important roles of other non-Western significant figures. The focus of this paper will be on the
role of the Islamic Golden Age in the transmission of the Hindu-Arabic numeric system to
Western Civilization, as it is often the key transition period that is missed when telling this
historical narrative from the Eurocentric perspective.
The term Islamic Golden Age describes the era of from the eighth to fourteenth
centuries where the Islamic Empire flourished economically, scientifically and culturally. At
its height the empires territories included modern day Middle East, Northern Africa, Pakistan,
Central Asia, Spain and Portugal.
2
This paper aims to deconstruct the ideas of Eurocentric
diffusionism on the introduction of the Hindu-Arabic numeracy system to Europe, which
mainly focuses on the successes of Fibonacci. This will be done by examining the detailed
history of how the Islamic world adopted, improved and preserved knowledge of the Hindu-
Arabic system as it spread west from India. The roles and works of key the historical Islamic
figure Al-Khwarizmi will also be highlighted.

Western civilization, The West or the Occident, are all terms used to describe
European civilization from approximately the 1400s to the present day, with its cultural origins
being traced to the synthesis of traditions from the Judeo-Christian and Greco-Roman eras of
antiquity.
3
The concept of Western civilization extends beyond European borders and includes
countries and cultures that were heavily influenced by European civilization through
colonialization, immigration or influence. The concept of Eurocentric diffusionism is the
fundamental belief that Western civilization had unique historical advantages, often falsely
attributed to cultural, environmental or racial factors, which lead to its superiority over all other
civilizations throughout history. This term also extends to the erroneous belief that non-
European civilizations only experienced growth or knowledge through the transmission of
knowledge from Western civilization.
4
These beliefs have permeated themselves throughout
contemporary society, such that even mathematics, which some may consider the most
unbiased area of studies, is also subject to these influences. If one were to believe some of the
traditional historical scholars of mathematics, it would be that all significant mathematical
discoveries came from Europe and were slowly adopted by the rest of the world by the way of
Eurocentric diffusionism. While in reality there are numerous instances of similar
mathematical concepts being discovered in different civilizations independently throughout
time. Despite this, these achievements are often solely attributed to mathematicians associated
with Western culture.
The more one delves into the history of maths it becomes less clear where
ideas truly came from and who they can be accurately attributed to, but one can be certain that
all civilizations have in some shape or form of practiced mathematics.

Our modern base ten numeration system known today as the Hindu-Arabic numeral
system is one of the most substantial developments in mathematics. What makes it so useful is
how it is comprised of only nine symbols, with zero as a placeholder and implementation of
decimal fractions. Any number can be represented by different combinations of only ten
symbols, while the place value system makes it easy to tell the magnitude of a number at a
glance just by looking at the number of digits it has. The place value also allows for vertical
arithmetic to become much more efficient (e.g. addition, subtraction, long division and long
multiplication etc.). This is a much simpler and ergonomic method compared to previous
numeration methods in the ancient world that were much less intuitive. With this numeracy
system, performing calculations became much more efficient, which then paved the way for
higher mathematics such as calculus and analysis.
6
The Hindu-Arabic system originates from India. The earliest record of its use dates
back to around the fourth century BC, and it originally only used the symbols for one to nine
without the decimal fraction system. The use of zero as a place value holder came later and
gradually became widespread over the next few centuries until the ninth century when it had
truly become commonplace across societies using the Hindu-Arabic system.
7
The first
evidence of Indian numerals making contact with the West is from a Syriac book from 662 AD
by a Syrian Nestorian Bishop called Severus Sebokht. He writes how he learned of Indian
numeration and found that it was much more logical than Roman numerals, and found it
distasteful that Greeks thought themselves the most superior in terms of mathematical
knowledge when their methods were not what he deemed the best out there. This demonstrates
that Indian numeration had reached parts of Europe before the Islamic Golden Age, but had

The Hindu-Arabic numeracy system was already completely adopted
by scholars and everyday people in the Islamic world by the eighth century. This was

9
At the height of the Islamic Golden Age, the empire included parts of Spain,
which is also one of the first ways the Hindu-Arabic system is speculated to have made its way
to Europe. The scholars who translated the works of Al-Khwarizmi studied in Spain in order
to obtain more knowledge from their civilization. It is also speculated that with the large trade
routes from the Islamic World to Europe that some merchants likely used the numeracy system

10
The first comprehensive text to deconstruct the Indian numeration system in the Islamic
world was Al-Khwarizmis Book of Addition and Subtraction According to the Indian
Calculation written around 825 AD. This book is similar to that of Fibonaccis Liber Abaci.
It also introduced the Indian numeracy system to the Islamic world by explaining how it works
and translates with regards to the existing systems, in addition to why it is useful. Scholars of
the Islamic world improved the system by introducing decimal fractions to represent specific
values of less than one. This was also described in Al-Khwarizmis work, and thus completes
the system of the modern base-ten Hindu-Arabic numeracy. This is why today it is called the
Hindu-Arabic in order to incorporate the contributions of both cultures, as well as to
differentiate from the Indian numeracy system before the Arabic decimal fraction
contribution.

Al-Khwarizmi collected and preserved mathematical works from India, China, Greece
and Persia by translating them to Arabic and building off of their knowledge to develop new
mathematical concepts. His comprehensive work on Indian numeration served essentially the
same purpose as Fibonaccis Liber Abaci. Both works provided a way to translate the
existing numeracy systems to Hindu-Arabic numerals and introduced them to the greater
populations. Al-Khwarizmis work was written about four hundred years before Fibonacci was
born, and was only translated to Latin sometime in the twelfth century. Despite being in
circulation around the same time as Liber Abaci it did not seem to have the same cultural
impact considering the mathematical concepts were similar. This is likely because only
scholars at the time who studied the Hindu-Arabic numeral system would understand it which
was quite rare. Regardless, Al-Khwarizmis work was the first stepping stone in the Westward
migration of Hindu-Arabic numeration, and popularized it across the Islamic Golden Age.
Fibonacci lived in the middle ages. He was born in 1170 and died sometime between
124050. Despite being Italian, he was mainly educated in North Africa where his father
worked as a diplomat, and also frequently travelled through Egypt, Syria, Greece, Sicily and
Provence. It was there he learned from his Muslim teachers of the Hindu-Arabic numeral
system, Indian computational techniques, and algebraic works of Al-Khwarizmi from Hisb
al-jabr walmuqablah (in English, The Compendious Book on Calculation by Completion
and Balancing).
12
Fibonacci was exposed to both cultures and mathematics quickly saw the
benefits of one system over the other as he described in the introduction to Liber Abaci. He
aimed to take what he had learned overseas and have Europe follow in similar suit

When critically looking at the historical evidence, it would seem that Fibonacci did not
miraculously discover the importance of Hindu-Arabic numerals then decide to publish a work
on it. Rather, it seems that he had spent a significant portion of his life exposed to another
cultures more effective numeracy system and had decided to make an effort to bring this
efficient system to the West. Particularly after learning of how his predecessor Al-Khwarizmi
did the same centuries before. Fibonaccis work was the most accessible to Latin speakers in
Europe at the time, but he was really the final step in knowledge transmission of the Hindu-
Arabic system. It is undeniable that Fibonacci made major historical contribution to the
popularization of Hindu-Arabic numerals in Europe through Liber Abaci. However the
narrative really begins with the gradual spread westward of the system from India, to the
Islamic world through Al-Khwarizmi, and eventually further west to Europe through the work
of Fibonacci. When considering these facts, the role of Al-Khwarizmi in the popularization of
the system in the Islamic Golden Age cannot be overlooked since it was here that Fibonacci
first learned of the system and how practical it could be in everyday society. Knowledge from
the rest of the world accumulated in the Islamic world at this time, then was slowly diffused
