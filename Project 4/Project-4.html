  1. Introduction

Israel has vastly surpassed other countries in the number of vaccine doses administered per 100 people from December 2020 to January 2021 for the COVID-19 disease (Ritchie, Hannah, et al.). Medical data gathered from these mass vaccination procedures is intended to be used by Pfizer-BioNTech to investigate the effectiveness of its vaccine on the population and to determine if a herd immunity effect occurs after reaching a certain percentage of vaccination coverage (Israeli Ministry of Health). With a gradual reduced spread of the virus due to increased immunity, the future vaccines will over time have lesser additional benefit, since the number of cases will not change as significantly for each new vaccination. Vaccinations in this sense can be modelled as an economic commodity which possesses an externality benefit of decreasing returns. This economic model, in conjunction with regression analysis, makes it possible to identify at which point in the vaccination process is herd immunity achieved.

  1. The Context and Data[1]

The Israeli government data is panel data consisting of repeated observations on the same age groups over time. Data measurements were taken at weekly intervals from August 30th 2020 to January 24th 2021 with 22 people observed in each age group. The weekly COVID-19 cases per million people is used as a measure of the disease’s spread, with its natural logarithm being the dependent variable of this study. The percentage of the population vaccinated with the first dose of Pfizer-BioNTech vaccine two weeks prior to the date recorded, is the first independent variable analyzed in this study. This two-week adjusted lag accounts for the time it takes for the immunity effects of the vaccines to exhibit themselves. The mean of COVID-19 cases over this time frame is 2598.15 cases per million people with a standard deviation of 2065.76. The mean of the lagged vaccinated population is 4.43% with a standard deviation of 15.22%. The population of Israel is approximately 9.3 million people as of December 2020 (United Nations, DESA, Population Division). Hence approximately 411,990 people have been vaccinated, and 24,162.80 cases per million people occur on average each week.[2]

  1. Regression Analysis

3.1 Simple Linear Regression

Equation 1 is the simple linear regression model for the percent of population who were vaccinated with the first dose two-weeks prior (lagvacc_per), regressed on the natural logarithm of weekly COVID-19 cases per million people (cases_log). ‘n’ is the number of observations and ‘R2’ is the adjusted R-squared value.

This model predicts that for every one percentage increase in the population who received their first dose vaccination two weeks prior, there is a 0.45% increase in the number of weekly COVID-19 cases per million people. This is equivalent to 4,500 new cases weekly[3], which is greater than the mean number of weekly cases so far. [4] For an effective vaccine, this seems like an unlikely result since it would be expected for infection to decrease with vaccination. This is a strong indication that there might be something incorrect with the model or other independent variables may be needed. The intercept in literal terms is representative of the natural logarithm of number of weekly COVID-19 cases per million people given no vaccinations. The model predicts zero vaccinations corresponds to 1510.20 weekly COVID-19 cases per million people[5]. This value is lower than the mean weekly cases calculated, which once again isn’t what is to be expected.[6] The intercept’s literal value realistically may not be representative of the true number of cases observed without vaccines. Viruses generally spread exponentially when there is no intervention, so it is unlikely that the case count would be constant over time. Yet it could represent the expected cases if current effective intervention of the Israeli people is reducing the exponential spread of the virus e.g. using masks, social distancing etc.

        This regression is used to construct a hypothesis test to determine if the vaccinations affect infection cases to begin with. This is a significant relationship to establish before investigating further into which specific level of vaccinations cause herd immunity. The null hypothesis is that the effect of vaccination on COVID-19 infections is zero, while the alternative hypothesis is that the effect is not zero. This statistically corresponds to the slope of the regression equaling zero, or not, respectively. The p-value for this test was 0.38 which is not significant even at the 10% level. [7]  This results in failure to reject the null hypothesis, but not accepting it either, so it cannot be determined if the vaccines affect cases by this model. The R-squared value of -0.00012 implies that nearly zero of the number of cases is explained by the vaccines.

Quite a number of the Gauss-Markov assumptions for simple linear regression assumptions do not hold. SLR.1[8] requires the two variables being regressed in the population model be linearly related. A scatterplot (Figure 1) of the log of weekly cases and the vaccination percentage shows majority points being clustered at zero. The data extends a few months before the vaccine distribution which began in December. This creates a disproportionate amount of zero values. Onwards from this cluster, there is semblance of a downward sloping linear relationship, with two possible outliers at the 10% and 20% lag vaccination percent. They have significantly lower COVID-19 cases than the rest of the data. SLR.1 does not appear to hold. However, it seems that if the majority of the zero clustered data were removed i.e., only take data from December 2020 onwards, the scatter plot would be linear enough that it would. SLR.2 requires that the samples from that data are randomized. The data gathered by the Israeli government is not entirely random. The data was designed with controls to evenly represent different age groups and areas of the Israeli population. 22 people were chosen for each age group, but chosen at random for each respective group (Israeli Ministry of Health); so overall the assumption holds. SLR.3 verifies that sample outcomes on the explanatory variable are not all the same value. Observing the value range of the vaccination lag cases (Table 1), clearly this assumption also holds. SLR.4 checks the zero-conditional mean assumption, and SLR.5 refers to homoscedasticity of the vaccination lag. Both assumptions are verified using a residuals versus fit plot (Figure 2), which is not a null plot in this instance. Thus we can conclude that these two assumptions do not hold. The points lack constant variation around the zero line, and closely resemble the shape of the Figure 1 scatter plot. With SLR.1, SLR.4 and SLR.5 not holding, it is not appropriate to use linear regression analysis for this set of data. This would explain the unexpected positive coefficient and lack of statistical significance found in the regression model. This also may indicate there are possible lurking variables that must be taken into consideration also when analyzing this data.

3.2 Multiple Linear Regression[9]

The nature of medical science is complex, since there are many possible confounding factors such as genetics, age, lifestyle etc. In this case timeframes also play a key role in understanding the effects of the vaccines. Their effects are not immediate and tend to take two weeks to exhibit themselves. By including these factors in a new multilinear regression model, a more accurate depiction of the vaccine’s effect can be constructed. Additional control variables of age and weekly time frame produced negative coefficients. This is consistent with the realistic context of the study, as we expect an increase in vaccinations to decrease the number of cases. In this new model almost all the results are statistically significant at the 1% level. Only the square of lag vaccination is significant at the 5% level, and none of the age group 20-29 estimations are statistically significant. The standard error for the lag vaccination coefficient is also significantly reduced, and the R-squared is much closer to one implying almost all of the variance of the cases is explained by the independent variables.

When considering the nature of age and timeframe in the context and data, it is understandable how these variables manipulated the results so considerably. Natural immunity changes with age. Elderly people in particular are more susceptible to the virus and experience aggravated symptoms, which is why Israel has been prioritizing this age group’s vaccination over others (Israeli Ministry of Health). This too would correspond to why the magnitude of the 80 or older age group coefficient is largest, with the coefficients tending to decrease as the ages get lower. If more people in the 80 or older group are being vaccinated than others, it makes sense that the decrease in cases is larger than that of the base group of age 0-14. Timeframe is also important in this particular dataset because vaccines were only administered in December 2020 which accounts for only the last two months of the six included in the data set. Prior to that, all vaccine data was zero which accounts for the large data cluster seen in Figure 1. By adjusting for these two lurking variables the new models appear a much more appropriate fit than the simple linear regression overall.

These lurking variables are also related to the omitted variable bias of the model. The correlation between each independent variable for regression 2 is positive (see Table 4). The coefficients for age are negative, and for the squared vaccination percentage they are positive (see Table 2). This means the squared vaccination variable has a positive bias on vaccination percentage, and that age has a negative bias on vaccination (see Table 4). This is coherent with the regression results. Notice the addition of the age variable in regression 2 decreases the of lagged vaccination coefficient, but the addition of the squared lagged vaccination variable in regression 3 increases it (see Table 2).

        In the third regression, the coefficient of the vaccination lag is negative, yet the coefficient for the vaccination lag squared is positive and both are statistically significant at the 5% level. This literally suggests that at low percentages of vaccinations, an additional vaccination would decrease the cases. Yet, the positive squared coefficient suggests that the effect must eventually be reversed. The number of cases could instead be modelled as a quadratic function of vaccinations. The semi-elasticity of cases with respect to the vaccination percentage would decrease as vaccination percentage increased, then reach a minimum point, and then the reverse would occur.[10] This mathematically corresponds to the negative gradient before the minimum point and positive gradient after of a convex quadratic curve. This minimum point must then represent the key vaccination percentage at which herd-immunity begins i.e., where the returns of each vaccine percentage begin to diminish. The minimum percentage of 1st dose vaccinated population with a two-week lag was calculated to be 69.1% (see calculation in Figure 3). This model thus predicts that herd immunity is achieved, by our economic definition of diminishing returns, when at the 69.1% level of vaccination in the population. This is equivalent to 6.4 million people in Israel.[11] 

        The Gauss-Markov assumptions for multiple linear regression correspond almost exactly to those of simple linear regression[12]. Considering the same data set is used, the only assumptions left to verify to justify the use of the multiple linear regression model is that of SLR.4 and SLR.5. The residual versus fit plots of these regressions appear quite evenly dispersed around the zero line, and lacking any clear patterns, which suggest a null plot (Figures 4, 5 and 6). The SLR.4 and SLR.5 assumptions would hold in these instances, unlike the simple linear regression model. Accounting for age and time in the new models most likely adjusted the large data cluster at zero seen in the simple linear regression scatter plot. It is thus appropriate to use a multiple linear regression model on this particular data.

  1. Limitations of Results

Regressions identify correlations between variables and predict outcomes, but do not explain how and why things happen. To say this study provides conclusive evidence for a causal relationship would be an incorrect interpretation of the information which regressions provide. Vaccines have been thoroughly researched and studied over centuries such that the scientific community has accepted the causal relationship between them and virus immunity. The Pfizer-BioNTech vaccine however is new, as is COVID-19. So, although this study and this regression cannot confirm a causal relationship, it would not be unreasonable to suggest that the vaccine is what is causing the reduction in cases. Further studies accounting for other lurking variables over longer periods would be necessary to absolutely determine if this were the case. With the data only coming from Israel, it could be that people of other countries may not respond to the vaccine in the exact same way due to genetic factors. Furthermore, the vaccine may become ineffective to mutated strains, or some people may have severe allergies to the vaccine. Lifestyle choices may also affect the vaccines effectiveness, such as smoking. COVID-19 is known to critically damage the respiratory system, so smokers may find that the vaccine may not exhibit itself with the same effectiveness as for non-smokers. Wealth would also affect who receives the vaccine, as those well-off tend to have superior social determinants of health overall in the long term. As a final point, this data relies on information given only the first dose, when it generally takes multiple doses for full immunity to be ascertained. The effects of multiple dosages versus just the first would be an interesting point of further study.

  1. Conclusion

The percentage of two-week lag in first vaccination dose of the Pfizer-BioNTech vaccine was modelled as a commodity experiencing diminishing returns. It was analyzed with multiple linear regression models using further pertinent independent variables confirmed by analyzing their omitted variable bias. This resulted in the level of herd immunity being identified as 69.1% of the population; equivalent to approximately 6.4 million people of Israel’s 9.3 million. According to most recent data (Mathieu and Ritchie)[13], as of February 19th 2021, 49.11% of the population has been vaccinated, or equivalently 4.5 million people. If this model is accurate then Israel is 70% complete with the process of achieving herd immunity and is much ahead of the curve compared to other nations.[14] With that momentum it would appear that herd immunity is likely to be achieved in 2021.
